## 9.2 CORE KNOWLEDGE BANK

### 9.2.1 What is a Service Contract?

**Definition**: A service contract is an arrangement where the **government hires a company** to perform exploration and/or production operations in exchange for a **fixed fee or performance-based payment**. The government **retains ownership** of all petroleum produced.

**Key Distinction from PSC/Concession**:

| Feature | Concession | PSC | Service Contract |
|---------|-----------|-----|------------------|
| **Ownership of petroleum** | Transferred to company | Shared/Cost oil + Profit oil | Retained by government |
| **Revenue basis** | Sales proceeds | Production share | Fee for service |
| **Risk allocation** | Full contractor risk | Shared risk | Government or contractor |
| **Contractor's interest** | Mineral rights | Contractual | None (pure) or limited (risk) |
| **Tax treatment** | Income tax on profits | Regime-specific | Fee taxed as income/WHT |

### 9.2.2 Types of Service Contracts

#### A. Pure Service Contracts

**Definition**: Contractor provides a **defined technical service** (e.g., drilling, seismic surveys) for a **fixed fee**, with **little or no exposure** to project performance or commodity price risk.

**Characteristics**:
- Fee is fixed or formula-based (not linked to production success)
- Contractor has no interest in discovered reserves
- Government bears all exploration and development risk
- Similar to hiring a contractor to build infrastructure
- Payment is for **services rendered**, not production

**Tax Treatment**:
- Fee taxable as **business income** in contractor's residence country
- May be subject to **withholding tax** at source
- No uplift or cost recovery mechanisms
- Transfer pricing rules apply to related-party arrangements

#### B. Risk Service Contracts

**Definition**: Contractor **assumes all or most financial and operational risks** for exploration and development, receiving a **prescribed fee per unit of production** only if commercially successful.

**Characteristics**:
- Fee is linked to actual production (e.g., per barrel)
- Contractor bears exploration risk (fee only if success)
- Contractor bears development capital risk
- Government retains title to petroleum
- Fee may include cost recovery element

**Tax Treatment**:
- Fee taxable as **business income**
- May qualify for cost recovery deductions
- Ring-fencing rules may apply
- Higher fee to compensate for risk assumption
- Iraq TSC model is primary example

## 9.4 TECHNICAL SERVICES TAX FRAMEWORK

### 9.4.1 UN Model Article 12A - Fees for Technical Services

**Background**: Introduced in 2017 UN Model Tax Convention update.

**Purpose**: Allows **source countries** to levy withholding tax on technical service fees paid to non-residents, even without a PE.

**Definition of "Fees for Technical Services"**:
> Payments of any kind to any person in consideration for the rendering of any service of a technical, managerial, or consultancy nature.

**Exclusions** (not covered by Article 12A):
- Payments to employees (Article 15)
- Payments constituting royalties (Article 12)
- Payments for teaching in an educational institution
- Payments for services for personal benefit of individual

**Key Features**:

| Element | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Taxing right** | Source country may tax |
| **Rate limit** | As agreed (commonly 10-15%) |
| **Method** | WHT at source |
| **Credit** | Residence country provides credit |
| **Make available** | Not required (unlike India model) |

### 9.4.2 UN Article 12AA (2025 Update)

**Development**: The 2025 UN Model introduced Article 12AA expanding source country taxation rights for service fees.

**Key Expansions**:
- Broader definition of covered services
- Enhanced source country rights even for routine services
- Greater alignment with developing country concerns
- Explicit coverage of digital service elements

**Exam Relevance**: Questions may reference "recent developments" in technical services taxation - know that UN Model continues expanding source country rights.

### 9.4.3 WHT Planning Strategies

**Objective**: Minimize withholding tax leakage while maintaining compliance.

**Strategies**:

| Strategy | Mechanism | Considerations |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Treaty shopping** | Route through favorable treaty jurisdiction | Anti-avoidance (LOB, PPT), substance requirements |
| **Service hub** | Centralize services in low-WHT location | Transfer pricing, operational substance |
| **Fee restructuring** | Convert services to non-WHT income | Substance over form, treaty characterization |
| **Grossing up** | Contractually shift WHT burden | Cash flow impact, commercial acceptability |
| **Credit planning** | Optimize foreign tax credit utilization | Credit limitation rules, excess credit positions |

## 9.5 IRAQ TECHNICAL SERVICE CONTRACTS (TSCs)

### 9.5.1 Background

**Context**: Iraq adopted the Technical Service Contract model after 2003 to develop its oil fields while maintaining full state ownership of petroleum resources.

**Types of Iraqi Service Contracts**:

| Type | Description | Key Features |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **TSC** | Technical Service Contract | First licensing round (2008), rehabilitation of existing fields |
| **DPSC** | Development and Production Service Contract | Larger fields, development focus |

### 9.5.2 Remuneration Fee Structure

**How it Works**:
- Contractor receives a **Remuneration Fee per Barrel (RFB)**
- Fee paid only on production above baseline
- Fee negotiated in competitive bidding
- Fee covers all contractor costs + profit margin

**Typical Fee Range**: $1.40 - $6.00 per barrel (bid rounds 2008-2012)

**Tax Treatment**:
```
IRAQ TSC TAX CALCULATION

Contractor receives RFB:        $5.00/barrel
Production above baseline:       1,000,000 barrels
Gross remuneration:             $5,000,000

Iraqi corporate income tax:      35%
Tax payable:                    $1,750,000

Net after-tax remuneration:     $3,250,000
Effective net fee per barrel:   $3.25
```

### 9.5.3 Recent Developments

**Investment Climate Challenges**:
- Poor payment record by government
- Bureaucratic delays
- Security concerns in some regions
- Currency restrictions

**Shift to PSAs**: Recent bid rounds have offered Production Sharing Agreements in addition to service contracts, responding to IOC preference for better fiscal terms.

## 9.6 TECHNICAL SERVICES AND JOAs

### 9.6.1 Joint Operating Agreement Service Charges

**Context**: In joint venture operations, the operator charges non-operators for:
- Overhead costs
- Technical services
- Project management
- Procurement services

**Transfer Pricing Issues**:

| Issue | Risk | Mitigation |
|-------|------|------------|
| **Overhead markup** | Excessive charges | Benchmark against industry rates |
| **Service allocation** | Disproportionate allocation | Use activity-based allocation |
| **Related party services** | Non-arm's length pricing | Document cost base, apply OECD guidelines |
| **Shared services** | Incorrect allocation keys | Use direct charge where possible |

### 9.6.2 Intra-Group Technical Services

**Common Services in Oil & Gas**:
- Seismic interpretation services
- Reservoir engineering services
- Drilling engineering support
- Procurement and logistics
- HSE management
- Finance and administration

**Transfer Pricing Methods** (from June 2025 exam):

| Method | Description | Application to Services |
|--------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Cost Plus** | Cost base + arm's length markup | Most common for routine services |
| **TNMM** | Net margin comparison | More complex arrangements |
| **CUP** | Compare to uncontrolled transactions | Where comparable exist |

**Cost Plus Application**:
```
COST PLUS CALCULATION FOR INTRA-GROUP SERVICES

Direct costs of service provision:    $800,000
Indirect costs (allocated):           $120,000
Total cost base:                      $920,000

Arm's length markup (e.g., 5%):        $46,000

Documentation required:
• Cost allocation methodology
• Benchmark study supporting markup
• Description of services provided
• Evidence of benefit to recipient
```

## 9.8 ERROR PREVENTION

### Common Exam Mistakes

| Error | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| Confusing service contract with PSC | Service contract = no production share | Government retains all production, contractor gets fee |
| Ignoring WHT on services | Services subject to source taxation | Always check WHT rates (domestic and treaty) |
| Missing Article 12A analysis | UN treaties have specific services article | Check treaty model (OECD vs UN) |
| Forgetting PE threshold for services | Service PE creates taxing nexus | Apply 183-day or similar threshold |
| Using wrong TP method | Not all methods suit all services | Cost Plus typically for routine services |
| Overlooking Iraq TSC structure | Unique model tested in exams | Know RFB per barrel mechanism |
| Mixing royalties and services | Different treaty articles apply | Use Template 9E for characterization |

## 9.10 CHAPTER SUMMARY

### Key Takeaways

1. **Service contracts** differ from PSCs/concessions - government retains all production, contractor receives fee
2. **Pure service contracts** = fixed fee, government risk, high WHT exposure
3. **Risk service contracts** = per-barrel fee, contractor risk, cost recovery possible
4. **UN Article 12A** allows source taxation of technical services (unlike OECD)
5. **Iraq TSCs** are the primary exam example of risk service contracts
6. **Transfer pricing** requires Cost Plus method documentation for intra-group services
7. **WHT planning** is critical - treaty position determines 0-25% rate differential
